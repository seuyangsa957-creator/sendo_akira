## Business Overview

Stryker is one of the world's largest medical technology companies, founded in 1941 and headquartered in Kalamazoo, Michigan. It operates across two reporting segments:

- **MedSurg & Neurotechnology (~60% of revenue)** — Surgical instruments, endoscopy systems, hospital beds and patient handling, emergency medical equipment, neurovascular stroke intervention, and craniomaxillofacial (CMF) products. FY2025 revenue: $15.65B (+15.8% YoY).
- **Orthopaedics (~40% of revenue)** — Knee and hip implants, trauma fixation, shoulder and extremity reconstruction, and the Mako SmartRobotics platform. FY2025 revenue: $9.47B (+4.3% YoY).

Revenue has compounded at ~9% annually over the past decade, growing from $10.7B (2015) to $25.1B (2025). GAAP EBITDA margins have expanded from ~19% in 2015 to ~24% in 2024.

## The Mako Flywheel

The crown jewel of Stryker's business is the **Mako SmartRobotics** platform — a closed, proprietary robotic system that only works with Stryker implants. The business model:

1. Sell a Mako robot (~$1.25M) at thin margin
2. Lock in recurring implant revenue (~$200–350K/system/year) because Mako only accepts Stryker implants
3. Each case requires a Mako Product Specialist (MPS) in the OR — deepening the hospital relationship
4. As the installed base grows (~3,000+ systems), implant pull-through compounds

~2/3 of all U.S. Stryker knee procedures are now done on Mako. The platform is expanding to spine (full commercial launch 2H 2025), shoulder, and hip revision. The new **Mako RPS** (handheld version, FDA-cleared Aug 2025, LMR Feb 2026) targets the ASC market.

## Pricing Power: MedSurg vs. Ortho

MedSurg enjoys **price-positive** trends driven by differentiated capital equipment that embeds into hospital workflows (beds, monitoring, IT integration). Switching costs are high. Ortho implants face structural ASP erosion of ~1%/year — they are bought by hospital procurement teams (VAC/GPO) who treat equivalent implants as commodities. Mako partially counters this by locking implants to the robot.

**Key competitive dynamic:** J&J MedTech and Zimmer Biomet are the most direct competitors. J&J's planned DePuy Synthes spin-off (expected 2026–2027) will create a ~$10B+ pure-play orthopedic company with VELYS as its flagship robotic platform. Zimmer Biomet holds ~33% U.S. knee market share vs. Stryker's ~29%, and has also acquired Monogram Technologies (semi-autonomous robot, expected commercial launch 2027).

**Intuitive Surgical** is the best comp for Stryker's Mako flywheel — same razor/razorblade logic, same installed-base-to-recurring-revenue structure, but in soft tissue robotic surgery (da Vinci) rather than orthopedics.
